## Abstract

Aerial manipulation for safe physical interaction with surrounding environments is gaining significant momentum in robotics research. In this paper, we present a **disturbance-observer (DOB)-based safety-critical control** for a fully actuated aerial manipulator interacting with both static and dynamic structures.

Our approach centers on a **safety filter** that dynamically adjusts the desired trajectory of the vehicle's pose, accounting for the aerial manipulator's dynamics, the disturbance observer's structure, and motor thrust limits. We provide rigorous proof that the proposed safety filter ensures the **forward invariance of the safety set** — representing motor thrust limits — even in the presence of disturbance estimation errors.

To demonstrate the superiority of our method over existing control strategies for aerial physical interaction, we perform comparative experiments involving complex tasks, such as pushing against a static structure and pulling a plug firmly attached to an electric socket. Furthermore, to highlight its repeatability in scenarios with sudden dynamic changes, we perform repeated tests of pushing a movable cart and extracting a plug from a socket. These experiments confirm that our method not only outperforms existing methods but also excels in handling tasks with rapid dynamic variations.

## Aerial Physical Interaction (APhI)

A key challenge in APhI is designing a controller that simultaneously handles two factors: the **external disturbance** generated by physical interaction between the vehicle and its environment, and the vehicle's **actuation limits** such as the motor thrust limit. Unlike prior work, our method requires **no interaction wrench measurement or estimation** from a force/torque sensor, making it suitable for aerial manipulators operating in windy environments.

## Contributions

- We design a **safety-critical controller** that can conduct various types of APhI with uncertain environments in the absence of interaction wrench measurement or estimation.
- We show that the **safety set** representing the motor thrust limits is **forward invariant** under the proposed safety filter.
- We validate that our controller **outperforms existing approaches** for APhI control through pushing and pulling experiments with both static and dynamic structures.

## Method

The aerial manipulator is configured with a fully actuated hexacopter (six tilted motors) and a rigidly attached robotic arm. We derive its Euler-Lagrange dynamic model and adopt a DOB-based control law that makes the system follow its desired trajectory under time-varying disturbances and model uncertainties.

<placeholder: Fig. 2 — Aerial manipulator hardware configuration with tilted motors, robotic arm, and coordinate frames>

### Control Framework

The overall framework consists of a **safety filter** and a **DOB-based control law**. Based on a target pose set by an external planner (e.g., joystick or ground station), the safety filter computes a desired pose and twist that conform to motor thrust constraints. The DOB-based control law then generates the motor thrust commands.

<placeholder: Fig. 3 — Overall controller diagram for safe APhI>

### Safety Filter

The safety filter modifies the desired pose trajectory based on the system model, the DOB structure, and the feasible range of motor thrust. We construct a safety set representing motor thrust limits using six control barrier functions, compensate for the DOB estimation error, and solve a **Quadratic Programming (QP)** problem to find the desired acceleration that keeps every motor within its thrust limits.

### Theoretical Guarantee

We prove that the safety set representing motor thrust limits is **forward invariant** under the combined system of the dynamic model, the DOB, the control law, the DOB error compensator, and the QP. This guarantee holds even in the presence of disturbance estimation errors.

## Experiments

We validate the proposed controller across four real-world scenarios. An industrial fan generates a wind blast to imitate a realistic disturbed environment. The aerial manipulator weighs 3.50 kg and is built on a DJI F550 frame with six tilted motors (α = 15°) and a rigidly attached robotic arm, running ROS Noetic on an Intel NUC with OptiTrack-based navigation.

### Scenario 1 — Pushing a Static Whiteboard

We compare the proposed method against two baselines: (1) without a safety filter, and (2) direct adjustment of the control wrench instead of the desired pose. Without any safety filter, the vehicle fails to maintain stability. The proposed method keeps the vehicle stable while pushing, with slightly less overshoot than the second baseline.

### Scenario 2 — Pulling a Firmly Attached Plug

Without a safety filter, the manipulator oscillates while pulling the plug. While both the second baseline and the proposed method achieve safe pulling, the second baseline exhibits large oscillation in motor thrust (and consequently in roll/pitch angles), whereas the proposed method remains smooth.

## Conclusion

We presented a safety filter built on a DOB-based control structure, designed to enhance pose-tracking performance while ensuring flight safety during physical interaction. We derived the Lagrange-Euler model of a fully actuated hexacopter with a rigidly attached robotic arm, developed a safety filter that accounts for motor thrust limits, external disturbances, and model uncertainties, and proved forward invariance of the safety set with respect to motor thrust limits. Real-world comparative and repeatability experiments confirmed the effectiveness of the approach. Future work may involve a hybrid controller to better manage abrupt dynamic changes, or extending the system for haptic-based teleoperation.
